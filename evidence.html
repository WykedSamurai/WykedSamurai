The Wyked Samurai Research Foundation

Research-backed. Human-centered. Experience-driven.

Wyked Samurai is built from HR coursework, social psychology, gamification research, people analytics, training strategy, and real operations experience. Its work is not about adding game mechanics to business for novelty. It is about using structured experiences to help people practice skills, demonstrate capability, understand one another, and build stronger workplace systems.

What this page is

This page makes the evidence behind Wyked Samurai visible. It translates school-based research, HR coursework, psychology reflection, and professional experience into a public-facing research foundation.

The goal is to show that Wyked Samurai is not only a creative brand. It is a developing professional framework grounded in human resources, workplace behavior, ethical technology, and experiential learning.

Core position

People grow through experience. Organizations improve through evidence. Technology should strengthen human judgment rather than replace it.

Wyked Samurai uses structured scenarios, reflection, evidence, and human-centered systems to support learning, development, and better workplace decisions.

Evidence Area 01

Gamification and experiential learning

What the coursework showed

Penny Carter's IDS 401 research examined gamification through interdisciplinary lenses, including history, humanities, natural and applied sciences, and social science. The work defined gamification as the use of game design elements in non-game contexts and studied how these systems can shape motivation, participation, learning, and work experiences.

The research also recognized that gamification is not automatically positive. Game mechanics can raise concerns about manipulation, fairness, surveillance, cultural fit, and employee autonomy.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

This research supports Wyked Samurai's focus on structured experience rather than surface-level rewards. The goal is not to add points and badges to workplace problems. The goal is to design meaningful scenarios where people can practice communication, judgment, leadership, collaboration, reflection, and problem-solving.

The strongest takeaway is that gamification must be ethical, culturally aware, and people-centered.

Evidence Area 02

HR strategy and workforce planning

What the coursework showed

The hiring strategy coursework used Wyked Samurai Consulting as an HR and organizational development case model. It included labor market analysis, recruiting strategy, structured interviews, skills-based assessments, onboarding, compliance, diversity recruiting, and workforce planning.

The project framed Wyked Samurai Consulting around leadership development, employee engagement, workplace culture improvement, and innovative training strategies.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

This gives Wyked Samurai a practical HR foundation. It shows that the concept has been developed using real HR language and organizational planning, not only creative worldbuilding.

For companies, owners, and recruiters, this matters because it connects the brand to hiring, onboarding, compliance, employee development, and business needs.

Evidence Area 03

Retention, engagement, and workplace culture

What the coursework showed

The employee retention strategy coursework focused on employee values, engagement surveys, stay interviews, career development, mentorship, workplace flexibility, communication, burnout reduction, and workplace well-being.

The work emphasized that retention requires both financial and non-financial strategies, including flexible total rewards, development pathways, listening sessions, and a culture where employees feel valued.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

This supports Wyked Samurai's people-first direction. The work is not only about training activities; it is about creating systems that help employees stay engaged, see a future, feel supported, and grow over time.

It also strengthens the case for scenario-based development because workplace culture is shaped by repeated behavior, communication, trust, and leadership choices.

Evidence Area 04

Training, onboarding, and stakeholder communication

What the coursework showed

The training communication coursework explained that HR must communicate training initiatives differently depending on the audience. Leadership needs strategic value and return on investment. Managers need team performance and reinforcement. Individual contributors need practical benefits and personal development.

This matters because good training does not succeed through content alone. It succeeds through buy-in, communication, support, and practical application.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

Wyked Samurai's experiential learning concepts need to be presented differently to executives, managers, employees, recruiters, and owners. This research supports a more professional communication strategy.

The same scenario can be described as ROI, leadership development, skill practice, culture repair, onboarding support, or employee growth depending on the audience.

Evidence Area 05

Social psychology, DEI, and emotional intelligence

What the coursework showed

The PSY 355 reflection connected social psychology to leadership, workplace relationships, diversity, equity, inclusion, conformity, group behavior, cognitive dissonance, social influence, implicit bias, stereotype threat, growth mindset, and emotional intelligence.

The work emphasized self-awareness, empathy, open communication, psychological safety, and fairer decision-making in hiring, development, and workplace culture.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

Scenario-based learning can model group pressure, leadership choices, communication habits, bias, trust, collaboration, conflict, and psychological safety. These are not abstract ideas. They are everyday workplace forces.

This strengthens Wyked Samurai's case because it shows that structured role-based experiences can help people practice the human side of work.

Evidence Area 06

AI ethics and human-centered technology

What the coursework showed

The HRM 300 learning journal examined AI and automation in HR, including data privacy, algorithmic bias, fairness, employee trust, and the risk of dehumanizing employees when important interactions are handled only by machines.

The work emphasized that HR professionals must balance technology with empathy, communication, policy design, and employee advocacy.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

Wyked Samurai approaches technology as a support for human judgment, not a replacement for it. AI and digital systems should improve clarity, access, and evidence-based development while preserving fairness, empathy, transparency, and trust.

This is especially important for Fabled Compass and any future Wyked Samurai tools because professional growth systems must protect people from being reduced to scores alone.

Evidence Area 07

People analytics and evidence-based decisions

What the coursework showed

The people analytics coursework included turnover calculations, workforce trend interpretation, and visual explanation of employee data. It showed how HR professionals can use data to identify patterns and support better decisions.

Turnover, engagement, and workforce data help organizations understand what is changing and where intervention may be needed.

How it supports Wyked Samurai

Evidence-based development is central to Wyked Samurai. The work values measurable patterns, observed behavior, documentation, reflection, and structured proof of capability.

This connects directly to Fabled Compass and to any system that helps people show what they can do instead of relying only on resumes or impressions.

Methodology

How the research becomes practice

Wyked Samurai uses structured, scenario-based experiences informed by HR, psychology, and gamification research to help people practice communication, leadership, judgment, collaboration, and problem-solving in ways traditional training often cannot.

Traditional resumes and interviews often capture claims, summaries, and impressions. Structured experiences can reveal decision-making, adaptability, communication patterns, problem-solving style, emotional intelligence, and teamwork in action.
